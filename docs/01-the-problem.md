# Tier 1 — The Problem

> Derived from Tier 0. The sharp version — not "people misread texts," but the exact thing Aida and no other tool is built to solve.

## Problem statement

For people on the neurodivergent spectrum, two failures break written relationships — and they compound.

**Problem 1 — Emotional blindness.** The emotional layer of communication is inaccessible, inward and outward. They cannot perceive their own emotion (somatic signal, no label) and cannot read others' subtext or tone. Critically, **the absence of readable intent does not stay neutral — it fills with worst-case intent.** Unread emotion defaults to threat: an ambiguous message becomes assumed malice, pushing the person into a paranoid, defensive state. Meanwhile their own directness is misread by others as rudeness. They wound and are wounded without seeing either happen.

**Problem 2 — Emotional memory won't encode.** (Anchor: TBI.) The sharpest framing, in his words: **"I can save people's pictures, names, and phone numbers. I cannot save their emotions."** Facts are storable; the emotional layer is not. What fails to persist is **the emotional state a person was in, and the emotional state they left in him** — per person, across time. That, not the facts, is the real problem.

**The mechanism — snapshot, not video.** A sharp emotional uptick burns a single still **snapshot** (which can survive for years), then **the emotion resets** and he moves on: *sharp emotion → snapshot → reset.* No continuous episodic recall; no video. So **memory encoding is gated by emotional salience — and emotional salience is exactly what Problem 1 removes.** An event with no sharp emotion is never stored. He reconstructs events by inference from physical evidence (*a new mouse on the desk → I must have gone to Best Buy*), not by remembering them.

**The window is hypothetical** — ~3 hours is his own rough self-measurement; sometimes 6. The reliable fact: **by the end of the day he does not remember the start of it** — the meetings, the people met, the plans discussed, the work he accomplished that morning. Stand-up meetings are hell: he reads **his own notes from yesterday as if a stranger wrote them** — an older instance of himself who did the work. He knows it was him only because no one else has access to his computer; he holds vague snapshots of having been there. For relationships, the arc never persists: every conversation restarts cold.

**What he uses today, and why it isn't enough.** Notes, diaries, reminders, calendar events, an inner-thought notebook, a photo gallery — all of it stores **facts**. RENA (Renamon) shares her memory with him automatically, but that is *her* perspective and *her* opinions — not his own emotional record of a person. Nothing he has holds the **emotional** layer in *his* voice.

**What this demands of Aida — the emotional bank account.** Aida must be two things at once: an emotional **translator** (reads what they mean now) and an emotional **bank account** (stores the emotion he cannot sustain). It holds a **layered emotional ledger per person, both directions** — the impression *they* left on him, and the impression *he* left on *them*. So the next time he meets someone from the past, the translator reads them against that layered emotional history, and the impression is already there. The point is not the words. In his words: **"I will remember *them* — not their words. THEM."**

**Why Problem 1 is *upstream* of Problem 2.** Subtext can only be read as *deviation from a person's baseline*, which requires memory of that person — and that memory fails to form *because* the emotional salience that would encode it is missing (Problem 1). It's one loop: no emotional read → no salient memory → no baseline → no emotional read. **So Aida's emotional tag does double duty: the read he needs in the moment, *and* the salience that makes the moment storable. The translator and the memory-harness are the same act.**

## What makes it hard (the real difficulty)

Subtext is **relational and historical, not lexical.** "Fine" is neutral from one person and a wall from another. Generic sentiment analysis reads text in isolation and is therefore structurally incapable of reading subtext at all. The hard problem is maintaining a faithful, evolving **per-person model** — emotional baseline, history, theory-of-mind — and reading each message against it. A memory-and-modeling problem, not an NLP problem. Second hard part: surfacing the read without deepening paranoia or making the person trust themselves less.

## Scope — in and out

**In scope:** 1:1 written communication held over time; reading incoming messages; checking outgoing messages before they land; building and keeping a per-person model; an inward somatic→emotion practice as a secondary mode.

**Out of scope (for now):** group dynamics; live spoken conversation / audio tone; clinical treatment of the conditions; lie-detection. (Phase 2 widens surface via a Chrome extension — not the core.)

## North star

> **Aida reads the space between what people say and what they mean — and remembers who they are — so people who can't perceive emotion or hold relational memory can still know, and be known by, the people they talk to.**
